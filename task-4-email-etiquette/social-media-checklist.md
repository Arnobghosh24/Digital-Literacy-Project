**Social Media Do’s and Don’ts Checklist**



✅ **The Do’s (Responsible Use)**

Do curate your privacy settings regularly to ensure only intended audiences can see your personal posts.



Do fact-check information before sharing or reposting to avoid spreading "fake news" or misinformation.



Do use social media for professional networking (e.g., LinkedIn) to connect with alumni and industry leaders.



Do pause before posting: Ask yourself if you would be comfortable with a future employer or professor reading the content.



Do give credit to original creators when sharing digital art, photography, or written work.



❌ **The Don’ts (Risky Behavior)**

Don’t post "vent" or "burn" posts about professors, classmates, or internship supervisors, as these can easily be screenshotted.



Don’t share sensitive personal data, such as your home address, student ID number, or current precise location (geo-tagging).



Don’t engage with "trolls" or participate in cyberbullying, even if you feel you are defending yourself.



Don’t post photos involving illegal substances or underage drinking, which can violate university codes of conduct.



Don’t click on suspicious links in DMs, as these are often phishing attempts designed to compromise your accounts.



Project Report: Digital Communication Reflection

The Scenario: The "Reply All" Career Disaster



A common but devastating example of poor digital communication occurs during the internship hiring process. In one documented case, a student received a rejection email and intended to forward it to a friend with a frustrated, unprofessional comment about the company’s "boring culture" and "clueless recruiters." Instead of hitting "Forward," the student accidentally hit "Reply All," sending the insult directly to the entire HR department.



**The Impact and Resolution**



This mistake immediately blacklisted the student from that organization and damaged their reputation among recruiters who move between companies. To have handled this differently, the student should have adhered to the "24-hour rule"—waiting a day to process emotions before responding to professional correspondence. Furthermore, using digital tools for emotional venting is inherently risky. A better approach would have been to discuss their frustration privately in person or over a phone call, ensuring their professional digital footprint remained untarnished.
